## 3. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This complaint alleges that Innovative Medicines Canada (IMC) and its member companies engaged in coordinated anti-competitive conduct in violation of sections 45 (conspiracy) and 79 (abuse of dominant position) of the *Competition Act*, R.S.C., 1985, c. C-34.

Between 2019 and 2022, IMC coordinated a lobbying campaign with a single objective: to block or weaken regulatory reforms that would have introduced price competition for patented prescription drugs in Canada. The campaign succeeded. On June 17, 2022, the Minister of Health personally intervened to suspend the reforms two weeks before they were to take effect.

As a direct result of this coordinated conduct:
- Canadian prescription drug prices remain among the highest in the OECD
- Canadian patients and taxpayers continue to pay inflated prices
- Competition in the patented drug market was suppressed by regulatory capture

The factual pattern mirrors the *Trudeau II* ethics finding: a minister used their position to influence an independent body's decision to further private corporate interests. Here, the conduct also violates competition law because the private interests were advanced through coordinated action by dominant market players.

## 4. STATEMENT OF FACTS

### 4.1 The Reforms That Would Have Increased Competition

In 2019, Parliament amended the *Patented Medicines Regulations* to:
- Require actual net price disclosure (after rebates and discounts)
- Expand the basket of comparator countries to include lower-priced jurisdictions
- Set new price thresholds based on international benchmarks
- The reforms were scheduled to take effect **July 1, 2022**

These reforms would have introduced **price competition** into the Canadian patented drug market for the first time.

### 4.2 IMC's Coordinated Lobbying Campaign (2019-2022)

**Evidence:**
- **150+ registered lobbying meetings** with government officials (disclosed in IMC's public lobbying registry)
- **Single objective:** Block or weaken the PMPRB pricing reforms
- All major pharmaceutical companies acted through IMC as a **single coordinated entity**

**Key Personnel:**
- **Michel Fralick:** Senior Health Canada official (2008-2019) -> IMC Vice-President
- **Dr. Christiane Hamelin:** NSERC Executive -> IMC Vice-President
- **Terry Digby:** Pharma IP lawyer -> Chair of PMPRB (2012-2015)

### 4.3 The Ministerial Intervention

On **June 17, 2022**, Minister of Health Jean-Yves Duclos wrote to the PMPRB Chair directing the Board to:
- Suspend the new price benchmarks
- Delay implementation of the regulatory amendments
- Revert to the pre-reform pricing methodology

The letter's language was described as **"indistinguishable from pharmaceutical lobby talking points."**

The PMPRB complied. The reforms were suspended before taking effect and never implemented in their intended form.

### 4.4 The Paid Consultancy Arrangement

IMC paid Statistics Canada **$161,000** for reports on drug pricing. These reports were used in IMC's lobbying materials. This arrangement raises questions about whether government research was being used for private anti-competitive purposes.

### 4.5 Market Dominance

IMC member companies collectively control the vast majority of patented prescription drug sales in Canada. Canada has no universal pharmacare program and no single-payer bargaining system to countervail this dominance. As a result, Canadian drug prices are among the **highest in the OECD**.

## 5. LEGAL ANALYSIS

### 5.1 Section 45 — Conspiracy

> *"Every person commits an offence who, with a competitor, conspires, agrees or arranges to fix, maintain, increase or control the price for the supply of a product."*

**Application:**
- IMC is a trade association whose members are direct competitors in the patented drug market
- Through IMC, these competitors coordinated a campaign to **maintain high drug prices**
- The campaign had a single objective: prevent regulatory reforms that would lower prices
- This is not legitimate advocacy — it is coordinated action by competitors to control prices

### 5.2 Section 79 — Abuse of Dominant Position

> *"Where the Competition Tribunal finds that one or more persons have engaged in anti-competitive acts, it may order the person to cease the conduct, pay a fine, or take other corrective measures."*

**Application:**
- IMC member companies collectively hold a **dominant position** in the Canadian patented drug market
- Their coordinated lobbying to suppress price reforms constitutes **anti-competitive acts**
- The conduct had the effect of **preventing competition** from entering the market
- Canadian patients and taxpayers suffered harm through continued high prices

### 5.3 Section 46 — Foreign Directives (Alternative)

> *"Every person commits an offence who implements or gives effect to a foreign directive that has the effect of lessening competition in Canada."*

Some IMC member companies are foreign-owned (Pfizer US, Roche Switzerland, Novartis Switzerland, AstraZeneca UK/Sweden). If coordinated pricing directives originated from foreign headquarters, this section may also apply.

## 7. REQUESTED REMEDY

The complainant respectfully requests that the Commissioner of Competition:

1. **Initiate an investigation** into IMC's coordinated lobbying campaign (2019-2022)
2. **Determine** whether the conduct constitutes:
   - A conspiracy under section 45 of the *Competition Act*
   - An abuse of dominant position under section 79
   - Implementation of foreign directives under section 46
3. **Seek an order** from the Competition Tribunal if sufficient evidence is found, including:
   - Cease-and-desist order for coordinated lobbying to suppress competition
   - Administrative monetary penalty
   - Remedial measures to restore competitive pricing
4. **Recommend** to Parliament that lobbying by dominant market players be subject to competition law scrutiny
5. **Refer** evidence of ministerial intervention to the Conflict of Interest and Ethics Commissioner
